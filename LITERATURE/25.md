18. Analytical Synthesis
Kesharwani et al. (2021) is a narrative clinical review of diabetic retinopathy covering pathophysiology, risk factors, clinical presentation, diagnosis, and management. The article contains no computational, deep learning, or image analysis content whatsoever. Its epistemic weight for a dissertation on AI-based DR detection is negligible. The article suffers from serious methodological deficiencies: multiple references are incorrectly matched to their cited claims (at least six references appear entirely unrelated to the text assertions they are supposed to support), Wikipedia is cited as a source, and the conclusion contains a factual contradiction regarding the association between myopia and DR. The journal (JPRI) has been flagged for questionable editorial practices. This article neither strengthens nor weakens a preprocessing-dominance argument, as it does not engage with any computational methodology. It does not demonstrate cross-dataset robustness or contribute to benchmarking discourse. For a doctoral literature review, this paper should be classified as peripheral and should not be cited for any technical or methodological claims. If cited at all, it should be limited to the most general clinical context, and all specific claims should be verified against original, higher-quality sources.
