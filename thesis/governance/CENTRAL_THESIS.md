**Paradigmatic framing.** The conceptual foundation of this dissertation is a contrast between two paradigms for automated diabetic retinopathy diagnosis. Paradigm **P1** — the end-to-end CNN paradigm — treats preprocessing as ancillary data preparation that does not require methodological discussion; the deep network is taken to learn the relevant invariances directly from raw or minimally normalised pixels. Paradigm **P2** — the integrated preprocessing-CNN paradigm — treats preprocessing as an integral component of the model itself, on the grounds that it defines the feature space available to the network and therefore co-determines what the network can learn. Gulshan et al. (2016, *JAMA*) is designated in this dissertation as the **canonical representative of P1**, by virtue of its observed methodological practice (preprocessing details deferred to the supplementary material; emphasis on architecture, data scale, and training protocol). The present work is the **paradigmatic instantiation of P2**: it formalises an 8-stage preprocessing pipeline as a binding part of the model specification, places it under controlled experimental contrast (Experiment 1, configs A/C vs. B/D), and reports the result as an empirical contrast between two paradigms under otherwise matched conditions — not as a numerical comparison against Gulshan's reported figures (see SB-1.12, CFC-2.9, SIR-9 in INVARIANTS v5.3).

---

The proposed model is defined as a two-stage system: an 8-stage fundus image preprocessing pipeline followed by a CNN classifier. The preprocessing pipeline is an integral component of the model, not an external data preparation step — it defines the feature space available to the CNN. This integrated preprocessing-CNN system — comprising canonical flip, OD-fovea rotation normalization, FOV crop with isotropic resize and centered zero-padding, FOV mask generation as a 4th input channel, adaptive flat-field illumination correction (σ = 0.07 × FOV diameter), upgraded CLAHE enhancement in LAB color space with dual-constraint clip limit and stochastic application, and dataset-specific channel-wise normalization — reduces domain variability across fundus imaging devices and acquisition conditions while preserving diagnostically relevant retinal features, producing a statistically measurable and reproducible improvement in five-class diabetic retinopathy diagnostic performance compared to an equivalent architecture trained without preprocessing (stretch-resize + ImageNet normalize only, 3 channels). This improvement, validated primarily on EyePACS with cross-dataset transferability demonstrated on APTOS 2019 (G ≥ 0.85), clinical degradation resistance evaluated on IDRiD and Messidor-2, and robustness across diverse imaging devices (DDR, ODIR-5K, RFMiD capturing Canon, Topcon, Kowa, and Zeiss camera hardware), is further substantiated by Grad-CAM explainability analysis showing that preprocessing redirects CNN attention toward clinically relevant lesion regions, quantified by Attention–Lesion Overlap (ALO) as the primary metric and IoU as a secondary metric on IDRiD, with qualitative Grad-CAM overlays on a Kazakh clinical dataset. The pipeline thus constitutes a transferable, interpretable, and device-robust preprocessing regime for automated DR diagnosis under constrained computational conditions.
