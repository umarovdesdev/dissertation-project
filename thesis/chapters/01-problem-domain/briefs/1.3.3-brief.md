### CONTENT SPECIFICATION

**Section objective:** Survey the explainability methods relevant to CNN-based medical-image classification — the CAM → Grad-CAM → Grad-CAM++ activation-mapping lineage and the model-agnostic attribution methods (SHAP, LIME) and XAI surveys (LRP) — select Grad-CAM as the dissertation's primary interpretability tool for lesion-overlap evaluation, and bind the whole discussion to NC-14: these methods support interpretation, not clinical localization or validation of diagnostic reasoning.

**Argumentative spine (required):**
- **Thesis of this section:** Post-hoc explainability methods — chiefly the class-activation-mapping family (CAM, Grad-CAM, Grad-CAM++) — make a trained CNN's spatial evidence inspectable and provide a basis for asking whether a model attends to clinically plausible retinal regions; the dissertation adopts Grad-CAM for this purpose, but treats its outputs strictly as interpretability signals, never as clinical localization of pathology or as validation of the model's reasoning.
- **Reasoning chain:**
  1. Deep CNNs are opaque; explainability methods exist to expose what spatial evidence drives a classification (motivation; #59/#60 XAI surveys).
  2. The activation-mapping lineage localizes class-relevant image regions: CAM via global average pooling (#57), Grad-CAM generalizing it via gradients to arbitrary CNNs (#46), Grad-CAM++ refining localization and multi-instance handling (#58).
  3. Model-agnostic attribution methods (SHAP #61, LIME #62) and relevance-propagation approaches (LRP, via #60) provide complementary, non-spatial or surrogate explanations, but are not the dissertation's operational lesion-localization tool.
  4. The dissertation selects Grad-CAM because its spatial output can be compared against pixel-level lesion masks (IDRiD) to compute attention–lesion overlap — the basis of the ALO (primary) / IoU (secondary) metrics in Exp 4 — among the CAM family for which such overlap is naturally defined.
  5. Crucially, this overlap is an interpretability diagnostic, not a clinical localization: a high-activation region is gradient evidence, not a pixel-accurate lesion delineation, and a favorable overlap does not validate the model's diagnostic reasoning (NC-14, SB-1.11).
- **Conclusion / hand-off:** Establishes the explainability toolset and the Grad-CAM selection under the NC-14 boundary, completing the three methodological pillars (architecture §1.3.1, pretraining §1.3.2, explainability §1.3.3); hands off to §1.4, which turns from method components to the *systems* that assemble them — a critical analysis of existing automated DR screening systems.

### COUNTER-ARGUMENT & INHERITED LIMITATIONS

- **Counter-position to address:** A natural objection is that if Grad-CAM overlap with lesion masks is high, the model "has been shown to look at the lesions" — i.e., its reasoning is validated. The section must pre-empt this: high attention–lesion overlap is consistent with clinically plausible attention but does not establish that the model's decision is *caused* by lesion features, nor that activations delineate lesions at pixel accuracy; overlap is an interpretability diagnostic bounded by NC-14/SB-1.11. This is exactly why the dissertation frames Exp 4 as explainability evidence, not as clinical validation.
- **Inherited limitations (SIR-2):** all seven sources are general-domain (non-retinal) method references; their reported localization/attribution metrics are method properties on ImageNet-class data, not DR-lesion results.
