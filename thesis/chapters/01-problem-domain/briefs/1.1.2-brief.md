### CONTENT SPECIFICATION

**Section objective:** Establish that diabetic retinopathy screening must be delivered at population scale against a structural shortage of specialists and infrastructure, and that resource-limited settings (operationalized in OD-6) impose the deployment constraints under which an automated, decision-support DR classifier must operate — taking the early-detection imperative from §1.1.1 as given.

**Argumentative spine (required):**
- **Thesis of this section:** Effective DR control depends on periodic population-wide screening, but the human and infrastructural capacity to deliver it is structurally insufficient — especially in resource-limited and rural settings such as Kazakhstan — which establishes the demand for an automated, low-resource-deployable decision-support classifier operating within a physician-in-the-loop paradigm.
- **Reasoning chain:**
  1. The treatable therapeutic window fixed in §1.1.1 is realizable only through repeated screening of large diabetic populations, yet observed screening compliance is low (35–55%, #34).
  2. The bottleneck is structural: specialist supply is finite and geographically concentrated (≈1,200 ophthalmologists nationally in Kazakhstan; ~40% rural population; ~70% of rural residents with limited eye-care access — #22), so manual grading cannot scale to the screening demand.
  3. Automated screening is proposed in the cited literature as a workload-reduction mechanism (#34, #22), with portable/telemedicine delivery (Fundus on Phone ~$5,000; UK 80% coverage — #22) — but these are reported as third-party context/projection, not dissertation outcomes.
  4. Deployment in such contexts is resource-constrained; this section anchors the OD-6 operational definition and the physician-in-the-loop / decision-support boundary (SB-1.3) that bound every later deployment-oriented statement.
- **Conclusion / hand-off:** Establishes screening scale + specialist scarcity + resource-limited deployment context as the system-level motivation; licenses §1.2 to examine the technical precondition for automated screening to work in the field — namely, the quality of the images such screening actually produces.

**Required content elements:**
1. Screening as the operational route to the §1.1.1 therapeutic window; low compliance (35–55%, #34) as evidence of an unmet need.
2. Structural specialist scarcity and rural-access gap, Kazakhstan as the framing case (≈1,200 ophthalmologists; ~40% rural; ~70% limited access — #22, self-cited).
3. Automated/AI-assisted and telemedicine/portable screening as the cited response (workload reduction #34; UK 80% coverage, portable camera cost, IDx-DR/Remidio metrics — #22, #34) — all as third-party context.
