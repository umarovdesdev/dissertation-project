**Key concepts established:**
- DR control depends on systematic, repeated, population-scale screening (the early grades being asymptomatic), converting the §1.1.1 therapeutic-window imperative into a logistical/capacity problem.
- Observed screening compliance is low (35–55%, #34) → a large fraction of treatable disease goes undetected under current models.
- The bottleneck is structural specialist scarcity and rural-access gap, framed via Kazakhstan (≈1,200 ophthalmologists nationally; ~40% rural; ~70% rural with limited eye-care access — #22, self-cited).
- Automation is positioned in the cited literature as a workload-reduction mechanism, with telemedicine/portable delivery (UK 80% coverage; smartphone fundus camera ~$5,000; relayed IDxDR/Remidio/EyeArt operating points) — all third-party context.
- **OD-6 (resource-limited environment)** introduced as the binding deployment envelope (≥2 of: no GPU; <16 GB RAM; near-real-time throughput; limited connectivity).
- **SB-1.3** decision-support / physician-in-the-loop boundary fixed: the eventual system is an aid, not a specialist replacement.
- Screening-research data are geographically/demographically specific and dataset-bound (IDRiD = Indian population, #06/#10) → DGL-1 foreshadowed.

**Terms introduced (now available without re-definition):** population-scale screening, screening compliance, referable DR (clinical sense), resource-limited environment (OD-6), physician-in-the-loop / decision-support paradigm (SB-1.3), telemedicine DR screening, portable/smartphone fundus camera, dataset-bound generalization (DGL-1, as a named recurring constraint).

**Argument thread:** The system-level demand has been fixed — population-scale screening against a structural shortage of specialists, to be met by a low-resource-deployable, decision-support classifier. The active thread closes by making the precondition explicit: whether such a classifier works depends on the quality of the images that real-world, often non-specialist, screening produces.
