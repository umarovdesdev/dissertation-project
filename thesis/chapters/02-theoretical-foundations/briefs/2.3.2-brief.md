### CONTENT SPECIFICATION

**Section objective:** Establish the spectrum of transfer-adaptation strategies — from frozen-base (fixed-feature) use to progressive (two-stage) fine-tuning — show that theory and the candidate's prior DR experiments converge on progressive fine-tuning under domain shift, motivate the two-stage protocol, and situate domain-adversarial adaptation as a distinct, non-adopted alternative.

**Argumentative spine (required):**
- **Thesis:** Adaptation of transferred weights ranges from holding the backbone frozen (training only a new head) to progressively unfreezing and fine-tuning the upper layers; both transfer-learning theory and the candidate's prior diabetic-retinopathy experiments indicate that progressive fine-tuning yields more balanced, better-generalizing models than the frozen-base strategy under the natural-image→fundus domain shift, which motivates the two-stage fine-tuning protocol — while domain-adversarial adaptation remains a distinct representation-level alternative not adopted for the H-1 backbones.
- **Reasoning chain:**
  1. Two ends of the adaptation spectrum: frozen-base / fixed-feature extraction (backbone frozen, only a new classification head trained) vs progressive/two-stage fine-tuning (head trained first, then upper layers unfrozen and adapted). Disambiguate the glossary terms.
  2. Theory favors fine-tuning under domain shift: Yosinski et al. (2014) — fine-tuned transferred features can outperform target-only training, and freezing can incur co-adaptation/optimization penalties; Kornblith et al. (2019) — fine-tuning beat fixed-feature transfer in 179/192 cases — with the advantage largest where the target diverges from the source (the fundus case).
  3. The candidate's prior DR experiments empirically confirm this on the target task: the frozen-base model reached a test F1 of 0.62 while progressive fine-tuning reached 0.74 (+12 pp), with improved macro/weighted averages and a reduced train/test gap, though overall accuracy was unchanged at 0.80 (previously published results; #19/#21, one thread).
  4. The design therefore adopts a two-stage progressive fine-tuning protocol (formalized in §3.3.3), applied as a shared training method across the H-1 configurations (not an experimental IV — H-3 dropped). Domain-adversarial training (Ganin et al., 2016: a gradient-reversal layer learning domain-invariant features) is a distinct representation-level adaptation strategy relevant to device domain shift (§4.7) but is not the adaptation mode chosen here.
- **Conclusion / hand-off:** Progressive fine-tuning is the adaptation strategy, empirically supported and theoretically expected under domain shift; this completes the experimentally-exercised foundations (preprocessing, CNN, training, transfer). The chapter now turns to its one purely theoretical, simulation-only strand — the coupled thermal-optical model of fundus tissue — in §2.4.1. (In-domain SSL theory, §2.3.3, is deferred this pass.)
