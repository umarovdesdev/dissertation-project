### CONTENT SPECIFICATION

**Section objective:** Establish the contrast-enhancement lineage — global histogram equalization → adaptive (tile-local) histogram equalization → contrast-limited (clipped) adaptive histogram equalization — as the theoretical foundation for the V5 pipeline's contrast stage, and frame why a contrast-limiting mechanism is required for fundus imagery, without overclaiming that contrast enhancement universally improves downstream classification.

**Argumentative spine (required):**
- **Thesis of this section:** Global histogram equalization improves contrast by redistributing intensities but is unsuited to fundus images; locality is recovered by adaptive (tile-wise) histogram equalization at the cost of noise over-amplification; limiting the transformation slope by clipping the histogram (Pizer et al., 1987 — the CLAHE precursor) controls that over-amplification, establishing contrast-limited adaptive histogram equalization as the principled contrast foundation for the V5 pipeline — while empirical evidence shows its downstream effect is real but parameter- and architecture-dependent, not universal.
- **Reasoning chain:**
  1. Histogram equalization redistributes pixel intensities to approximately flatten/uniformize the intensity histogram, increasing global contrast (glossary Foundational Concept; #54 background).
  2. A single global mapping is dominated by the large low-intensity background of a circular fundus image and cannot adapt to spatially local, low-contrast lesions — motivating *adaptive* (region/tile-local) histogram equalization.
  3. AHE recovers local contrast but, in near-homogeneous regions, produces a steep transformation that over-amplifies noise; Pizer et al. (1987) showed that limiting the mapping slope is equivalent to clipping the histogram height and redistributing the clipped mass — the methodological origin of CLAHE.
  4. Retinal/medical preprocessing studies (#27 Hayati; #26 Shaout & Han; #30 Chakka; #23 self) show contrast enhancement materially changes perceived/downstream quality, but with weak or architecture-dependent evidence (Hayati: +5/+5/+2% on three CNNs but −12% on ResNet34; Shaout: 10-person survey; Chakka: no metrics) — so the lineage motivates a *controlled, parameterized* contrast stage rather than a presumption of universal gain.
- **Conclusion / hand-off:** The HE→AHE→clipped-AHE lineage fixes CLAHE as the contrast foundation and isolates the clip limit as the governing parameter; the precise dual-constraint clip-limit formalization adopted by the V5 pipeline is derived in §2.1.2.

**Required content elements:**
1. Definition and mechanism of global histogram equalization (intensity-redistribution via the CDF mapping), and why its global statistic is inadequate for fundus images (background-dominated histogram, no spatial adaptivity).
2. Adaptive histogram equalization (per-tile mapping) — recovers locality; its failure mode = noise over-amplification in low-variance regions (slope of the transform).
3. Contrast-limited / clipped AHE: Pizer et al. (1987) slope-limiting ≡ histogram-clipping + redistribution; the clip limit as the contrast-vs-noise control knob. Cite #54 (NOT #25). DGL-5 portability caveat at first parameter mention.

6. Explicit hand-off to §2.1.2 (dual-constraint clip-limit derivation) and forward note to §4.3 (CLAHE sensitivity sweep, H-2).
