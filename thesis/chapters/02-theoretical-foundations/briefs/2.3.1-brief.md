### CONTENT SPECIFICATION

**Section objective:** Establish the theory of feature transferability — its layer-dependence and its decay with domain distance — and use it to motivate the dissertation's two pretraining regimes (ImageNet vs in-domain ophthalmology-SSL) under the binding principle (DGL-6) that transfer to the fundus domain is empirically evaluated, never assumed.

**Argumentative spine (required):**
- **Thesis:** Feature transferability is real but bounded — lower CNN layers learn general, broadly transferable features while higher layers become task-specific, and transferability decays as source and target domains diverge; because the natural-image→fundus gap is large, ImageNet transfer to DR classification is a cross-domain shift that is not guaranteed, which (under DGL-6) motivates the dissertation's two pretraining regimes and the principle that transfer is evaluated empirically.
- **Reasoning chain:**
  1. Transferability is layer-dependent: lower layers transfer near-perfectly, higher layers specialize; transferability decreases with the distance between base and target tasks (Yosinski et al., 2014). Fine-tuning transferred features can generalize better than training on the target alone.
  2. Within natural-image transfer, source-model quality strongly predicts transfer (Kornblith et al., 2019: r=0.99 fixed features, 0.96 fine-tuning), fine-tuning beats fixed-feature transfer and accelerates convergence — but benefits diminish as the target diverges from ImageNet labels and as target data grows.
  3. The natural-image→retinal-fundus shift is exactly the kind of large domain distance Yosinski's principle predicts will degrade the transfer of higher, task-specific layers; the medical-imaging limited-label regime (Cheplygina et al., 2018) is the practical context, and semi-supervised transfer can reach useful DR performance from few labels (Arrieta et al., 2022) — transfer is useful but bounded.
  4. This motivates the H-1 composite design (DGL-6): the baseline arm initializes from ImageNet (a cross-domain transfer), the integrated arm from in-domain ophthalmology-specific self-supervised pretraining of the same CNN backbone (reducing the domain gap); neither is theoretically guaranteed and both are evaluated empirically. (In-domain SSL theory is developed in §2.3.3; the practical adaptation strategy in §2.3.2.)
- **Conclusion / hand-off:** Transferability is layer- and domain-dependent and empirically bounded; *how* transferred weights are adapted — frozen versus progressive fine-tuning — is the subject of §2.3.2.
