### CONTENT SPECIFICATION

**Section objective:** Establish the mechanics of convolutional feature extraction — convolution (local receptive fields, weight sharing), nonlinearity, and pooling (downsampling/invariance) composed into a learned feature hierarchy — and the residual/dense connectivity that makes deep hierarchies trainable, grounding the dissertation's CNN backbones and connecting pooling-driven small-feature loss to the preprocessing rationale.

**Argumentative spine (required):**
- **Thesis:** A CNN extracts a hierarchy of spatially-local features by composing convolution, nonlinearity, and pooling — gaining parameter efficiency and translation equivariance from weight-shared local filters — and the depth that makes this hierarchy expressive is made trainable by residual/dense connectivity; in fundus-lesion analysis the pooling-driven loss of small-feature resolution makes the feature-extraction design inseparable from the preprocessing that renders small, low-contrast lesions visible (model = preprocessing + CNN).
- **Reasoning chain:**
  1. Convolution applies learned filters over local receptive fields with shared weights, producing translation-equivariant feature maps with far fewer parameters than dense connection; AlexNet (#65) established the large-scale supervised CNN, with ReLU and pooling as core components.
  2. Pooling spatially downsamples, conferring local invariance and enlarging the effective receptive field; stacking conv→nonlinearity→pooling composes low-level edge/texture detectors into progressively abstract representations (the feature hierarchy; FIG-2.2).
  3. Greater depth raises representational capacity but induces the optimization-degradation problem; residual learning (#66) via identity shortcuts eases optimization and enables >100-layer networks (ResNet-50 a dissertation backbone); dense connectivity (#67) strengthens feature propagation and reuse at high parameter efficiency (an alternative connectivity lineage).
  4. In retinal-lesion tasks, repeated pooling discards the small-lesion features that define early DR; EAD-Net (#08) limits pooling and adds attention specifically to preserve microaneurysm-scale features, and still nearly fails on microaneurysms — illustrating that feature extraction must be matched to the small/low-contrast targets the preprocessing stages (flat-field, contrast-limiting) work to preserve.
- **Conclusion / hand-off:** Feature extraction produces the representation on which the classifier head and loss operate; §2.2.2 turns to the loss functions that train this representation under the severe class imbalance of DR data.

**Required content elements:**
1. Convolution: local receptive field, weight sharing, feature map, translation equivariance, parameter efficiency (vs dense).
2. Nonlinearity (ReLU, #65) and pooling (max-pooling, downsampling, invariance, receptive-field growth) — glossary-canonical definitions.
3. The feature hierarchy (low→high abstraction); FIG-2.2 schematic.
4. Depth and trainability: degradation problem; residual learning (#66, identity shortcuts) → ResNet-50 backbone; dense connectivity (#67, feature reuse/efficiency).
5. Fundus-specific tension: pooling vs small-lesion preservation (#08 EAD-Net), tied to model = preprocessing + CNN.
